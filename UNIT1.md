# GROUP A: 1-MARK QUESTIONS (SHORT ANSWER)

**Introduction & Types of Research:**

**Q. 1. What is the primary objective of conducting research?**
**Main Answer:** The primary objective of research is to discover answers to questions through the application of **scientific procedures** and to find out the hidden, undiscovered truth.

**Q. 2. State one main difference between Research Methods and Research Methodology.**
**Main Answer:** **Research Methods** refer to the specific tools and techniques used to conduct a study, whereas **Research Methodology** is the systematic, theoretical science of studying *how* research is done scientifically.

**Q. 3. What is Descriptive Research?**
**Main Answer:** **Descriptive Research** includes surveys and fact-finding enquiries with the major purpose of describing the state of affairs exactly as it exists at present (ex post facto research).

**Q. 4. What is the fundamental goal of Basic (Fundamental) Research?**
**Main Answer:** The fundamental goal of **Basic Research** is the gathering of knowledge for knowledge's sake and the formulation of a generalized **theory** without any immediate commercial application.

**Q. 5. Give one example of Applied Research.**
**Main Answer:** An example of **Applied Research** is developing a specific vaccine to cure a newly emerged viral outbreak in a target population.

**Q. 6. What is the difference between Quantitative and Qualitative research in terms of data?**
**Main Answer:** **Quantitative** research relies on the measurement of **numerical data** and statistical analysis, while **Qualitative** research relies on **non-numerical data** like words, observations, and underlying motives.

**Q. 7. Define Empirical Research.**
**Main Answer:** **Empirical Research** is data-based research that relies strictly on observation or experimentation, coming up with conclusions capable of being verified by further observation.

**Q. 8. What is Conceptual Research?**
**Main Answer:** **Conceptual Research** is related to abstract ideas or theories, commonly used by philosophers and theoretical scientists to develop new concepts or reinterpret existing ones.

**Q. 9. Mention any one essential criterion of "Good Research".**
**Main Answer:** Good research must be **Systematic**, meaning it is structured with specified steps to be taken in a logical sequence.

**Research Problem & Literature Review:**

**Q. 1. What is a "Research Problem"?**
**Main Answer:** A **Research Problem** is a specific issue, difficulty, contradiction, or gap in knowledge that a researcher aims to address or investigate systematically.

**Q. 2. Why is defining a research problem considered the most important step in research?**
**Main Answer:** It is the most important step because a well-defined problem clearly dictates the **scope, methodology, and direction** of the entire study ("A problem well-defined is half solved").

**Q. 3. What is a Primary Source of literature? Give one example.**
**Main Answer:** A **Primary Source** provides direct, original, first-hand evidence or data about an event or research topic. **Example:** Original peer-reviewed research articles.

**Q. 4. What is a Secondary Source of literature? Give one example.**
**Main Answer:** A **Secondary Source** interprets, analyzes, or summarizes primary sources. **Example:** Systematic review articles or textbooks.

**Q. 5. What is a Monograph?**
**Main Answer:** A **Monograph** is a highly detailed, specialized, and heavily documented written study on a single specialized subject or an aspect of it, usually by a single author.

**Q. 6. How does a Patent serve as a source of literature review?**
**Main Answer:** A **Patent** serves as a vital source by providing highly detailed, original technical specifications of state-of-the-art inventions that are rarely published in academic journals.

**Q. 7. Name two popular academic research databases.**
**Main Answer:** Two popular academic research databases are **Scopus** and **IEEE Xplore**.

**Q. 8. What does identifying a "Research Gap" mean?**
**Main Answer:** Identifying a **Research Gap** means finding an unexplored area, an unresolved contradiction, or an unanswered question within the existing body of literature.

**Q. 9. What is a Critical Literature Review?**
**Main Answer:** A **Critical Literature Review** is not merely a summary, but a rigorous, objective analysis and evaluation of existing literature to identify strengths, weaknesses, and contradictions.

**Hypothesis:**

**Q. 10. Define a "Working Hypothesis".**
**Main Answer:** A **Working Hypothesis** is a tentative assumption or a proposed explanation made in order to draw out and test its logical or empirical consequences.

**Q. 11. Is it mandatory for all types of research (e.g., exploratory) to have a working hypothesis?**
**Main Answer:** **No**, it is not mandatory; exploratory and descriptive research designs often operate without formal hypotheses, focusing instead on data gathering.

**Q. 1. Differentiate between Research Methods and Research Methodology with suitable examples.**
**Introduction:**
Students and novice researchers often use the terms methods and methodology interchangeably, but they are fundamentally distinct concepts in academia.

**Main Answer:**
| Feature | Research Methods | Research Methodology |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Definition** | The specific tools, techniques, or processes used to collect and analyze data. | The systematic, theoretical analysis of the methods applied to a field of study. |
| **Scope** | Narrow; refers only to the practical execution of the study. | Broad; encompasses the overarching strategy, theoretical framework, and rationale. |
| **Objective** | To find solutions to the research problem via data extraction. | To logically explain *why* a particular method was chosen over others. |
| **Example** | Using a Likert-scale questionnaire, conducting a laboratory titration, or running an ANOVA test. | Explaining why a Quantitative, survey-based approach was theoretically the most valid way to answer the research question. |

**Conclusion:**
In short, research methods are a subset of research methodology. Methodology is the blueprint, while methods are the individual tools used to construct the building.

**Q. 11. Write a short note on the "Web as a source" for literature review. What are the precautions one must take while using web sources?**
**Introduction:**
The Internet has revolutionized literature reviews, providing instantaneous access to millions of global publications. However, its unregulated nature requires extreme caution.

**Main Answer:**
*   **The Web as a Source:** The web offers massive repositories, institutional repositories, pre-print servers (like arXiv), and databases (like Google Scholar) that make tracking citations and downloading papers highly efficient.
*   **Precautions to Take:**
    1.  **Authority (Credibility):** Anyone can publish on the web. Researchers must ensure the source is a recognized, peer-reviewed journal or reputable institution, not a predatory journal or a blog.
    2.  **Accuracy and Objectivity:** Web articles may be sponsored or heavily biased. Information must be cross-verified with traditional, peer-reviewed primary sources.
    3.  **Volatility:** Web pages frequently disappear or change (Link Rot). Researchers must properly cite the date of access and rely on permanent Digital Object Identifiers (DOIs).

**Conclusion:**
While the web is an incredibly powerful tool for literature discovery, researchers must apply rigorous critical evaluation (like the CRAAP test: Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose) before citing web sources.

**Q. 12. How does a researcher identify "Gap Areas" from existing literature and research databases?**
**Introduction:**
A **Research Gap** is an unanswered question or an unresolved contradiction in a field. Identifying this gap is the primary mechanism for proving a new study's originality.

**Main Answer:**
Researchers systematically identify gap areas through the following methods:
1.  **"Future Scope" Sections:** Actively reading the *Conclusion* and *Future Work* sections of highly cited, recent papers, where authors explicitly state what they failed to cover.
2.  **Systematic Mapping:** Creating a matrix of existing literature (comparing variables, methods, and populations) to visually spot an intersection that has never been tested (e.g., a drug tested on adults but never on children).
3.  **Identifying Contradictions:** Finding two reputable papers that draw entirely opposite conclusions on the same topic. The gap is the need for a definitive study to resolve the conflict.
4.  **Database Analytics:** Using tools in Scopus or Web of Science to track citation networks and discover a highly relevant, recently emerged sub-topic with very few publications.

**Conclusion:**
Finding a gap area ensures the proposed research is not just a redundant exercise, but a necessary brick added to the wall of global knowledge.

**Q. 13. What is a Working Hypothesis? Explain the steps involved in developing a valid working hypothesis.**
**Introduction:**
A **Working Hypothesis** is a tentative, testable assumption made by a researcher to guide the logical and empirical execution of their study.

**Main Answer:**
*   **Concept:** It is called "working" because it is subject to modification as the investigation proceeds. It acts as the focal point around which the entire research design is built.
*   **Steps for Development:**
    1.  **Initial Observation & Review:** The researcher observes a phenomenon and conducts an exhaustive literature review to understand the theoretical background.
    2.  **Identifying Variables:** The researcher explicitly identifies the independent variable (cause) and the dependent variable (effect).
    3.  **Formulating a Statement:** Creating a clear, declarative, and precise statement predicting the relationship between the variables (e.g., "Increased sleep duration improves memory retention").
    4.  **Ensuring Testability:** The researcher refines the statement to ensure that it can be conclusively proven or disproven using available statistical tools and empirical data.

**Conclusion:**
A valid working hypothesis narrows the research down from a broad, vague problem into a sharp, measurable, and highly specific objective.

### Question 2: Focus on the Research Problem (8 + 7)

**(a) What is a Research Problem? Detail the systematic process of defining and formulating a research problem. (8)**

**Main Answer:**
**Definition:**
A **Research Problem** is a specific area of concern, an identified gap in current knowledge, or a specific contradiction that requires a systematic, scientific investigation to resolve. It is the interrogative statement that the entire research project seeks to answer.

**Systematic Process of Defining and Formulating a Research Problem:**
1.  **Identify a Broad Subject Area:** The researcher begins with a generalized area of personal interest and academic competence (e.g., "Artificial Intelligence in Healthcare").
2.  **Dissect the Broad Area into Sub-areas:** The broad topic is broken down into manageable, specific sub-topics (e.g., "AI in Radiology", "AI for Patient Data Security", "AI for Surgical Robotics").
3.  **Select a Specific Sub-area:** The researcher selects the single sub-area that is most novel, fascinating, and feasible to study (e.g., "AI in Radiology for detecting lung tumors").
4.  **Raise Research Questions:** The researcher brainstorms specific questions within this sub-area (e.g., "How does AI accuracy compare to human radiologists in detecting early-stage lung tumors?").
5.  **Formulate Objectives:** The questions are translated into declarative, actionable objectives (e.g., "To mathematically compare the false-negative rate of Algorithm X versus Human Doctors in lung tumor detection").
6.  **Assess Feasibility:** The researcher rigorously checks if they have the budget, data access (hospital records), and time required to achieve these objectives.
7.  **Final Formulation:** If feasible, the objective is polished into a highly specific, final Research Problem statement.

**(a) Elaborate on the significance of a Literature Review in the research process. How does it prevent the "reinvention of the wheel"? (7)**

**Main Answer:**
**Significance of a Literature Review:**
A literature review is a comprehensive survey and critical evaluation of all previously published scholarly works related to a specific topic. It is the absolute foundation of any credible research.
1.  **Establishing Context:** It familiarizes the researcher with the historical background, current state-of-the-art, and key controversies within the field.
2.  **Identifying Research Gaps:** It highlights exactly what has *not* been done, allowing the researcher to formulate an original problem statement.
3.  **Methodological Guidance:** By reviewing past studies, a researcher learns which experimental designs succeeded and which statistical tools failed, helping them optimize their own methodology.

If a researcher spends three years conducting an experiment, only to discover later that a German scientist published the exact same findings a decade ago, the entire project is academically worthless (it cannot be published). A rigorous literature review prevents this catastrophic waste of resources. By mapping exactly what has already been proven, it ensures the researcher begins their work precisely at the boundary of human knowledge, pushing it forward rather than repeating past efforts.

**(c) Explain the concept of a "Critical Literature Review". How is it different from a simple summary of previous papers? (4)**

**Main Answer:**
**Concept:** A **Critical Literature Review** is an objective, thorough, and highly analytical evaluation of existing research. It does not just accept published papers as absolute truth; it interrogates them.

**Difference from a Simple Summary:**
*   A **Simple Summary** merely lists what past authors did (e.g., "Author A found X. Author B found Y. Author C used method Z."). It is descriptive and passive.
*   A **Critical Review** synthesizes and interrogates the literature. It identifies methodological flaws in past studies (e.g., "Author A found X, but their sample size was statistically insignificant"). It contrasts conflicting studies (e.g., "While Author A found X, Author B found Y, a contradiction likely caused by Author B's failure to control for temperature").
It actively carves out the logical justification for why the new proposed research is absolutely necessary to fix these past errors.

### Question 4: Focus on Web Sources, Databases, & Hypothesis (6 + 5 + 4)

**(a) Discuss the role of modern research databases (like Scopus, Web of Science, Google Scholar) and the Web in searching for literature. What criteria should be used to evaluate the credibility of web sources? (6)**

**Main Answer:**
**Role of Modern Databases:**
Modern research databases (Scopus, Web of Science, IEEE Xplore, PubMed) have digitized and centralized global human knowledge. 
*   They provide advanced boolean search operators to instantly filter millions of papers.
*   They track citation metrics, allowing researchers to instantly see how many times a paper has been cited, which serves as a proxy for its scientific impact.

**Criteria for Evaluating Web Credibility (The CRAAP Test approach):**
Because the open web is unregulated, sources must be strictly evaluated:
1.  **Authority:** Who is the author? Are they affiliated with a recognized university or research institute? Is the journal peer-reviewed?
2.  **Accuracy:** Is the data supported by hard evidence? Are there verifiable citations to primary sources?

4.  **Objectivity (Purpose):** Is the website trying to sell a commercial product or push a political bias? Objective academic research must be free of commercial influence.

**(b) What is a Research Gap? Explain the systematic approach a researcher takes to identify gap areas from a cluster of literature. (5)**

**Main Answer:**
**Research Gap:** A research gap is a specific question or problem that has not been addressed by existing studies, or an area where current results are contradictory and inconclusive.

**Systematic Approach to Identification:**
1.  **Extensive Reading:** Read the most recent, highly cited review articles in the target domain to grasp the current boundary of knowledge.
2.  **Targeting "Future Work":** Scrutinize the *Conclusions* and *Limitations* sections of original research papers. Authors are scientifically obligated to state what their study failed to achieve, directly handing future researchers a gap.
3.  **Methodological Mapping:** Create a spreadsheet tracking the variables, methodologies, and populations used in the last 50 papers. 
4.  **Spotting the Void:** Analyze the spreadsheet to visually spot intersections that are empty. For example, if 50 papers tested a structural algorithm on text data, but none tested it on image data, the application to image data represents a clear, justifiable research gap.

**(c) What is a Working Hypothesis? Explain its development process and its role in guiding the research design. (4)**

**Main Answer:**
**Working Hypothesis:** It is a tentative, testable prediction or assumption about the relationship between two or more variables, formulated to draw out and test logical consequences.

**Development Process:**
It is developed *after* the literature review. The researcher synthesizes existing knowledge to make an educated, logical guess about what their own experiment will reveal. (e.g., "If variable X is increased, then variable Y will decrease").

**Role in Guiding Research Design:**
The working hypothesis is the mathematical anchor of the study. 
*   It tells the researcher exactly what data needs to be collected (and prevents the collection of irrelevant data).
*   It dictates the specific statistical tools required. (e.g., A hypothesis looking for a correlation requires a Pearson correlation test, while one looking for a difference requires a T-test).
*   Ultimately, the entire research design exists solely to either accept or reject this single working hypothesis.

**(b) What are the common errors made by beginners while selecting a research problem? (5)**

**Main Answer:**
Novice researchers frequently ruin their studies before they even begin by making the following problem-selection errors:
1.  **Selecting an Overly Broad Problem:** (e.g., "The impact of the Internet on Society"). This is impossible to measure. Problems must be razor-sharp and narrow.
2.  **Selecting an Overly Trivial Problem:** Choosing a problem that has already been solved decades ago, leading to zero contribution to the field.
3.  **Ignoring Resource Constraints:** Selecting a brilliant problem but realizing halfway through that it requires a $100,000 electron microscope or 10 years to complete.
4.  **Selecting Controversial/Subjective Topics:** Beginners often pick highly politicized or morally subjective topics that cannot be measured objectively using empirical data.
5.  **Lack of Literature Base:** Selecting a problem so obscure that absolutely no background literature exists, making it impossible to formulate a grounded hypothesis.

**(c) "All research is an attempt to find answers to questions, but not all question-answering exercises are research." Justify this statement highlighting the scientific nature of research. (5)**

**Main Answer:**
**Justification:**
Humans ask and answer questions constantly in daily life (e.g., "Why is my car not starting?" -> "Because the battery is dead"). While this is an attempt to find an answer, it is **not research**.

Research is fundamentally defined by its **scientific, systematic, and rigorous nature**.
1.  **Lack of Intuition:** Everyday question-answering relies on guesswork, intuition, and common sense. True research absolutely rejects intuition; it relies strictly on verifiable, empirical data.
2.  **Systematic Methodology:** Research requires a pre-planned, rigid blueprint (the research design). Answering everyday questions is usually chaotic and unstructured.
3.  **Generalizability:** Everyday answers solve one specific, isolated problem. Scientific research aims to formulate generalized theories or universal laws that apply to broad populations.
4.  **Peer Scrutiny:** Research must be documented transparently so it can be replicated and validated by the global scientific community.

**Conclusion:** Therefore, unless the process of answering a question is systematic, empirical, objective, and aimed at expanding the boundaries of human knowledge, it cannot be classified as scientific research.
