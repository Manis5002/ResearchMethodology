# GROUP A: 1-MARK QUESTIONS (SHORT ANSWER)

**Research Ethics:**

**Q. 1. What is Research Ethics?**
**Main Answer:** **Research Ethics** refers to the specific moral principles, rules, and professional standards of conduct that guide researchers to ensure honesty, integrity, and respect for human and animal subjects during a study.

**Q. 2. Name the three main types of research misconduct (Hint: FFP).**
**Main Answer:** The three main types of research misconduct are **Fabrication, Falsification, and Plagiarism (FFP)**.

**Q. 3. What is Data Fabrication?**
**Main Answer:** **Data Fabrication** is the unethical act of completely making up, inventing, or forging data, observations, or results and recording or reporting them as genuine.

**Q. 4. What is Data Falsification?**
**Main Answer:** **Data Falsification** is the unethical manipulation of research materials, equipment, or processes, including omitting or altering conflicting data, so that the research record does not accurately represent the actual findings.

**Q. 5. What is the full form of IEC or IRB in the context of human research?**
**Main Answer:** IEC stands for **Institutional Ethics Committee**, and IRB stands for **Institutional Review Board**.

**Q. 6. What is the full form of IAEC?**
**Main Answer:** IAEC stands for **Institutional Animal Ethics Committee**.

**Q. 7. What is "Informed Consent" in human subject research?**
**Main Answer:** **Informed Consent** is the voluntary, documented agreement by a human subject to participate in a study, given only after they have been fully informed of the study's purpose, procedures, risks, and their absolute right to withdraw at any time.

**IPR & Patent Law:**

**Q. 8. Expand the abbreviation IPR.**
**Main Answer:** IPR stands for **Intellectual Property Rights**.

**Q. 9. What is a Patent?**
**Main Answer:** A **Patent** is an exclusive legal right granted by a sovereign state to an inventor, giving them the monopoly to make, use, or sell an invention for a limited period (usually 20 years) in exchange for public disclosure of the invention.

**Q. 10. Can a mere mathematical formula or abstract idea be patented?**
**Main Answer:** **No**, mere mathematical formulas, abstract scientific theories, or discoveries of natural laws cannot be patented.

**Q. 11. What is a Copyright?**
**Main Answer:** **Copyright** is a legal right that grants the creator of an original literary, artistic, musical, or dramatic work exclusive rights to its use, reproduction, and distribution.

**Q. 12. Give an example of a work protected by copyright.**
**Main Answer:** Examples include **academic textbooks, software source code, architectural blueprints, and musical compositions**.

**Q. 13. What is a Royalty in the context of IPR?**
**Main Answer:** A **Royalty** is a legally binding payment made to an IP owner (licensor) by another party (licensee) in exchange for the ongoing right to use their patented invention, copyrighted work, or franchise.

**Q. 14. What does TRIPS stand for?**
**Main Answer:** TRIPS stands for **Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights**.

**Q. 15. Which international organization administers the TRIPS agreement?**
**Main Answer:** The **World Trade Organization (WTO)** administers the TRIPS agreement.

**Scholarly Publishing:**

**Q. 16. What does the acronym IMRAD stand for?**
**Main Answer:** IMRAD stands for **Introduction, Methods, Results, And Discussion**, which is the standard organizational structure for empirical research papers.

**Q. 17. What is Plagiarism?**
**Main Answer:** **Plagiarism** is the unethical appropriation of another person's ideas, processes, results, or words without giving them proper academic credit (citation), presenting it as one's own original work.

**Q. 18. What is "Self-Plagiarism"?**
**Main Answer:** **Self-Plagiarism** (or text recycling) occurs when an author reuses massive chunks of their own previously published and copyrighted work in a new paper without proper citation or publisher permission.

**Q. 19. What is the difference between a Citation and an Acknowledgement?**
**Main Answer:** A **Citation** formally credits the specific intellectual source of a scientific idea or data point within the text, whereas an **Acknowledgement** thanks individuals or funding agencies for non-authorship technical, financial, or administrative support.

**Q. 20. What does "Reproducibility" mean in scientific research?**
**Main Answer:** **Reproducibility** means that independent researchers using the exact same methods, data, and analytical tools described in a published paper can duplicate the study and achieve the exact same mathematical results.

**Q. 21. Who holds the primary "Accountability" for the integrity of a published research paper?**
**Main Answer:** The **Corresponding Author** holds the primary administrative and ethical accountability for the paper's integrity and communication with the journal.

**Q. 1. Discuss the major ethical issues in conducting research. Briefly explain Fabrication, Falsification, and Plagiarism (FFP).**
**Introduction:**
Scientific progress relies entirely on the absolute trustworthiness of the research record. Ethical breaches severely damage public trust, waste funding, and can cause direct harm (e.g., in medical research). The gravest ethical violations are collectively known as FFP.

**Main Answer:**
1.  **Fabrication:** This is the act of completely inventing or "making up" data or results and recording them. *Example:* A researcher failing to run a clinical trial, but generating a spreadsheet of fake patient recovery metrics to get published.
2.  **Falsification:** This involves manipulating research materials, equipment, or actively tampering with legitimate data. *Example:* Deleting statistical outliers that do not fit the desired hypothesis or digitally Photoshopping Western Blot images to make protein bands look clearer.
3.  **Plagiarism:** The theft or misappropriation of another person's intellectual property, ideas, or exact text without providing formal citation, passing it off as original work.

**Conclusion:**
Committing any form of FFP is considered academic fraud. It leads to the immediate retraction of published papers, loss of research grants, and often the termination of the researcher’s academic career.

**Q. 2. Write a short note on the role and necessity of Ethical Committees for animal and human research.**
**Introduction:**
To prevent the horrific abuses of human and animal subjects seen in historical experiments (e.g., the Tuskegee Syphilis Study), modern academic institutions are legally mandated to operate strict Ethical Committees.

**Main Answer:**
*   **Institutional Review Board (IRB) / Human Ethics Committee:**
    *   *Role:* Reviews all proposed research protocols involving human participants *before* the study begins.
    *   *Necessity:* It ensures that the risks to human subjects are minimized, that the anticipated scientific benefits outweigh the risks, and that rigorous **Informed Consent** procedures are implemented, protecting vulnerable populations.
*   **Institutional Animal Ethics Committee (IAEC):**
    *   *Role:* Reviews and approves all research involving the breeding, housing, and experimentation on animals.
    *   *Necessity:* It strictly enforces the **3Rs Principle**: **Replacement** (using non-animal models if possible), **Reduction** (using the minimum number of animals necessary for statistical validity), and **Refinement** (minimizing pain, suffering, and distress during procedures).

**Conclusion:**
Ethical committees serve as the ultimate gatekeepers, ensuring that scientific curiosity never supersedes fundamental human rights and animal welfare.

**Q. 3. Why is "Informed Consent" critical when conducting research involving human subjects? Explain the key elements of an informed consent form.**
**Introduction:**
**Informed Consent** is the cornerstone of modern medical and social research ethics. It ensures the fundamental human right to autonomy and bodily integrity.

**Main Answer:**
**Why it is critical:**
It guarantees that human subjects are not treated merely as "tools" for scientific progress. It prevents exploitation and ensures that participants are willingly taking part without coercion, deception, or fear of penalty.

**Key Elements of an Informed Consent Form:**
1.  **Purpose of the Research:** Clear, non-technical explanation of what the study aims to discover.
2.  **Procedures:** Exactly what the participant will be required to do and how long it will take.
3.  **Risks & Discomforts:** Honest disclosure of any physical, psychological, or privacy risks involved.
4.  **Benefits & Compensation:** What the participant or society gains, and any financial compensation provided.
5.  **Right to Withdraw:** An absolute, unconditional statement that the participant can stop the experiment and leave at any time without facing any penalty or loss of standard medical care.

**Q. 5. What are the three basic criteria for an invention to be patentable?**
**Introduction:**
Not all ideas or creations can be patented. To secure a legal monopoly over an invention, the applicant must mathematically and scientifically prove to the Patent Office that the invention satisfies three strict universal criteria.

**Main Answer:**
1.  **Novelty:** The invention must be entirely new. It cannot have been published, demonstrated, sold, or known to the public *anywhere in the world* prior to the date of the patent application. (This is why researchers must patent *before* publishing their papers).
2.  **Non-Obviousness (Inventive Step):** The invention must involve a technical advancement that is not glaringly obvious to a "person having ordinary skill in the art" (PHOSITA). Simply combining two known tools without producing a surprising new synergy is not patentable.
3.  **Industrial Utility:** The invention must be practically useful and capable of being manufactured or used in an industry. A hypothetical time machine that cannot actually be built lacks utility.

**Q. 6. Explain the concept of Commercialization of Intellectual Property. How does an inventor earn Royalty?**
**Introduction:**
A patent in a filing cabinet produces no wealth. **Commercialization** is the process of bringing patented intellectual property to the market to generate financial profit.

**Main Answer:**
*   **The Concept:** Because universities or individual inventors usually lack the mass-manufacturing capabilities or marketing budgets of mega-corporations, they commercialize their IP by transferring rights to corporate entities.
*   **Earning Royalties via Licensing:** Instead of selling the patent entirely, the inventor grants a **License** (legal permission) to a company to manufacture the invention. In return, the company pays a **Royalty**. 
*   **Mechanism:** A royalty is typically a strict, legally binding percentage of the gross or net sales revenue. For example, if a university licenses a patented microchip to Samsung, they might stipulate a royalty of $1 for every smartphone sold globally containing that chip. This provides a continuous stream of passive income to fund further research.

**Q. 7. Write a short note on the Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights (TRIPS) agreement. What is its significance globally?**
**Introduction:**
The **TRIPS Agreement** is the most comprehensive and binding multilateral agreement on Intellectual Property, established by the World Trade Organization (WTO) in 1995.

**Main Answer:**
*   **The Concept:** Before TRIPS, IP laws varied wildly; a drug patented in the USA could be legally copied and sold for pennies in another country. TRIPS forced all WTO member nations to adopt a strict, uniform minimum standard of IPR protection.
*   **Global Significance:**
    1.  **Harmonization:** It brought global consistency, ensuring 20-year patent terms and automatic copyright protections across international borders.
    2.  **Dispute Settlement:** It integrated IP enforcement into the WTO's powerful dispute settlement mechanism, allowing countries to sanction nations that tolerate IP piracy.
    3.  **Encouraging Global Trade:** By guaranteeing that international patents will be respected, it gave tech and pharmaceutical companies the confidence to export cutting-edge technology to developing nations without fear of massive intellectual theft.

**Q. 8. Explain the IMRAD format for designing a research paper. What is the specific purpose of the 'Methods' section?**
**Introduction:**
**IMRAD** is the internationally recognized, standardized architectural structure used for writing empirical scientific research papers in peer-reviewed journals.

**Main Answer:**
*   **I - Introduction:** Explains *Why* the study was undertaken. It reviews existing literature, identifies the research gap, and states the hypothesis.
*   **M - Methods:** Explains *How* the study was conducted.
*   **R - Results:** Explains *What* was found, presenting raw data using statistics, tables, and graphs without subjective interpretation.
*   **A - And**
*   **D - Discussion:** Explains *What it Means*. It interprets the results, contrasts them with past studies, and states the study's limitations.

**Purpose of the 'Methods' Section:**
The specific purpose of the Methods section is to guarantee **Reproducibility**. It must be written with the meticulous detail of a recipe book (specifying exact equipment models, chemical concentrations, and statistical software versions) so that any independent scientist globally can repeat the exact experiment and verify the truth of the results.

**Q. 9. Define Plagiarism. Discuss any three different types of plagiarism (e.g., Direct, Mosaic, Accidental, Self-plagiarism).**
**Introduction:**
**Plagiarism** is the most frequent ethical violation in academia. It is defined as the appropriation of another person’s text, ideas, or figures without giving appropriate credit, portraying the work as original.

**Main Answer:**
1.  **Direct (Verbatim) Plagiarism:** The most severe form. It involves copying a block of text word-for-word from someone else's paper without using quotation marks or citing the source.
2.  **Mosaic (Patchwork) Plagiarism:** Occurs when a researcher borrows phrases from a source without quotation marks, or finds synonyms to replace a few words (poor paraphrasing) while keeping the exact identical sentence structure and meaning of the original author.
3.  **Self-Plagiarism:** Occurs when an author reuses massive portions of their *own* previously published papers in a new publication. Because publishers usually hold the copyright to published papers, reusing the text without citing the old paper violates copyright law and artificially inflates the author's publication count.

**Q. 10. How can a researcher avoid plagiarism? Discuss the role of citations and reference management.**
**Introduction:**
Avoiding plagiarism requires meticulous tracking of literature and an absolute commitment to academic integrity during the writing phase.

**Main Answer:**
*   **Strategies to Avoid Plagiarism:**
    1.  **Proper Paraphrasing:** A researcher must read the source, completely internalize the concept, close the source document, and rewrite the idea entirely from scratch using their own unique sentence structure.
    2.  **Using Quotation Marks:** If an author's exact words are so perfect they must be used, the text *must* be enclosed in quotation marks ("...") alongside an immediate citation.
    3.  **Applying Plagiarism Checkers:** Running the final draft through software like **Turnitin** or **iThenticate** before submission to catch accidental similarities.
*   **Role of Citations and Reference Management:**
    Every borrowed idea must be accompanied by an in-text citation. To manage hundreds of sources without accidentally misattributing an idea, researchers use **Reference Management Software (like Mendeley, Zotero, or EndNote)**. These tools automatically store PDFs, link them to notes, and perfectly format citations into the final document, eliminating accidental academic theft.

**Q. 11. Differentiate between Citation and Acknowledgement in a research paper. Who should be included in the acknowledgement section?**
**Introduction:**
While both Citations and Acknowledgements give credit to others, they serve vastly different academic, intellectual, and administrative functions in a paper.

**Main Answer:**
| Feature | Citation | Acknowledgement |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Target** | Published intellectual ideas, specific data, or methodologies. | People, institutions, or funding agencies. |
| **Nature of Contribution** | Direct intellectual or theoretical foundation for the research. | Logistical, financial, or minor technical support. |

**Who should be Acknowledged?**
The acknowledgement section is strictly for individuals who helped the research but do not meet the strict criteria for full Authorship. This includes:
*   **Grant Agencies** that provided financial funding.
*   **Laboratory Technicians** who simply ran routine equipment.
*   **Colleagues** who proofread the manuscript for English grammar or provided a brief statistical consultation.

**Q. 12. What is Reproducibility in research? Why is the lack of reproducibility considered a "crisis" in modern science?**
**Introduction:**
**Reproducibility** is the foundational pillar of the scientific method. It means that if an independent research group follows the exact methodology detailed in a published paper, they will achieve the exact same mathematical results.

**Main Answer:**
*   **The Reproducibility Crisis:**
    In recent years, large-scale replication projects in psychology and cancer biology revealed that over 50% of landmark, highly-cited studies could *not* be reproduced when other scientists tried to repeat the experiments. 
*   **Why it is a "Crisis":**
    1.  **Wasted Billions:** Pharmaceutical companies spend billions developing drugs based on university research. If that baseline research is non-reproducible, the drug trials fail catastrophically.
    2.  **Erosion of Public Trust:** If scientific facts change daily because initial studies were poorly conducted, the public loses faith in science, vaccines, and policy guidelines.
    3.  **Root Causes:** The crisis is driven by intense pressure to publish ("Publish or Perish"), leading to *p-hacking* (manipulating statistics to find false positives), hiding negative results, and poorly written 'Methods' sections.

### Question 1: Focus on Scholarly Publishing & IMRAD (7 + 4 + 4)

**(a) Describe the IMRAD concept in detail. Explain what information goes into the Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion sections of a standard empirical research paper. (7)**

**Main Answer:**
**The IMRAD Concept:**
IMRAD is the universally accepted structural framework for organizing original scientific research articles. It ensures that complex data is presented in a logical, predictable, and highly readable sequence.

**Detailed Section Breakdown:**
1.  **Introduction (Why did you do it?):**
    *   Begins with broad background context to orient the reader.
    *   Conducts a concise literature review to identify the **Research Gap** (what is currently unknown).
    *   Ends with a sharp, specific declaration of the hypothesis and the primary objectives of the current study.
2.  **Methods (How did you do it?):**
    *   The most technical section, written specifically to guarantee **Reproducibility**.
    *   Details the study design, participant demographics, sampling techniques, equipment used (with manufacturer names), and the exact statistical software and tests used to analyze the data.
3.  **Results (What did you find?):**
    *   A purely objective, emotionless presentation of the raw data.
    *   Heavily utilizes tables, charts, and graphs to condense massive datasets.
    *   Reports the statistical significance ($p$-values) but *never* attempts to explain why the results occurred.
4.  **Discussion (What does it mean?):**
    *   The subjective interpretation of the results. Answers whether the initial hypothesis was proven or rejected.
    *   Contrasts the new findings with the past literature mentioned in the Introduction.
    *   Crucially, it must state the **Limitations** of the study (e.g., "Our sample size was too small") and suggest directions for future researchers.

**(b) Distinguish between the "Abstract" and the "Conclusion" of a research paper. (4)**

**Main Answer:**
| Feature | The Abstract | The Conclusion |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Location** | Placed at the very beginning of the paper (before the Introduction). | Placed at the very end of the paper (often merging with the Discussion). |
| **Purpose** | A standalone, miniature version of the entire paper designed to help readers decide if the full paper is worth reading. | To provide the final "takeaway" message and state the ultimate significance of the research. |
| **Content** | Summarizes *all four* IMRAD elements: 1 sentence on Introduction, 2 on Methods, 2 on Results, 1 on Discussion. | Does not summarize methods. Focuses entirely on the final deduced truth and its real-world application. |
| **Word Count** | Strictly limited (usually 150 to 250 words maximum). | Generally a single, impactful paragraph. |

**(c) What is the concept of Accountability in scholarly publishing? Discuss the responsibilities of the corresponding author versus co-authors. (4)**

**Main Answer:**
**Accountability in Publishing:**
By adding their name to a paper, a scientist is publicly staking their professional reputation on the absolute integrity, honesty, and accuracy of the data presented. If the paper is found to contain fabricated data, all authors are held academically accountable and may face severe disciplinary action.

**Responsibilities:**
1.  **The Corresponding Author:** 
    *   The designated "Captain" of the project. 
    *   Takes primary administrative responsibility for communicating with the journal editors, responding to peer-review critiques, and ensuring all publication ethics forms are signed. 
    *   Post-publication, they must provide the raw data to independent scientists if requested.
2.  **Co-Authors:**
    *   Must have made a "substantial intellectual contribution" to the study design, data acquisition, or analysis.
    *   Must actively draft or critically revise the manuscript.
    *   They share the ethical accountability. A co-author cannot claim ignorance if another co-author fabricated data in the shared manuscript.

### Question 2: Focus on IPR & Patent Law (7 + 4 + 4)

**(a) What is Intellectual Property Rights (IPR)? Discuss the Indian Patent Law perspective regarding what can and cannot be patented (mention non-patentable inventions). (7)**

**Main Answer:**
**Intellectual Property Rights (IPR):**
IPR refers to the legal rights granted to creators over the creations of their minds. They give the creator an exclusive right over the commercial use of their creation for a certain period of time, promoting innovation by guaranteeing financial reward.

**Indian Patent Law Perspective (The Patents Act, 1970):**
Indian law strictly balances rewarding inventors against public welfare (especially regarding pharmaceuticals and agriculture). For an invention to be patentable, it must possess **Novelty, an Inventive Step, and Industrial Applicability**.

**What CANNOT be Patented (Non-Patentable Inventions under Section 3):**
To prevent monopolies over basic human knowledge, Indian law explicitly excludes:
1.  **Frivolous Inventions:** Anything contrary to natural laws (e.g., a perpetual motion machine).
2.  **Contrary to Public Morality:** Inventions injurious to human, animal, or plant life (e.g., biological warfare agents).
3.  **Mere Discoveries:** Discovering a new living thing or non-living substance already existing in nature.
4.  **Mathematical or Business Methods:** Abstract algorithms, computer programs *per se*, or business models cannot be patented (though software can sometimes be copyrighted).
5.  **Agricultural Methods:** Any method of agriculture or horticulture.
6.  **Medical Treatments:** Methods of surgery, therapy, or diagnosis performed on humans or animals.
7.  **Evergreening (Section 3d):** Simply discovering a new property or new use for a known substance (heavily used to stop pharma companies from extending drug patents without making actual improvements).

**(b) Detail the steps involved in filing a patent and moving towards commercialization. (4)**

**Main Answer:**
1.  **Patent Search & Drafting:** An exhaustive global search is conducted to ensure the idea is truly novel. A patent attorney then drafts the "Patent Specification," rigorously defining the exact technical "Claims" of the invention.
2.  **Filing the Application:** The application (provisional or complete) is officially filed at the National Patent Office.
3.  **Publication & Examination:** After 18 months, the application is published globally. The inventor requests a formal examination. A patent examiner rigorously scrutinizes the claims against existing global patents.
4.  **Grant of Patent:** If all objections are resolved, the patent is officially granted, conferring a 20-year monopoly.
5.  **Commercialization:** The inventor approaches venture capitalists, startups, or corporations. The IP is then transferred into the marketplace via Licensing agreements, Spin-off companies, or outright sale of the patent.

**(c) Explain the concept of Royalty. How does licensing an IPR differ from completely assigning/selling it? (4)**

**Main Answer:**
**Royalty:** A continuous payment made to the legal owner of an IP (licensor) by a third party (licensee) for the right to use that IP. It is usually calculated as a percentage of gross or net sales generated by the invention.

**Licensing vs. Assignment:**
| Feature | Licensing (Renting the IP) | Assignment (Selling the IP) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ownership** | The original inventor **retains** the absolute ownership and title to the patent/copyright. | The original inventor completely transfers ownership and title. They no longer own it. |
| **Rights Granted**| Grants specific, restricted permission to use the IP (e.g., "You can manufacture this in India only for 5 years"). | Grants absolute, permanent, unlimited rights to the buyer globally forever. |
| **Financial Return**| Continuous, long-term passive income via ongoing **Royalties**. | A single, one-time massive lump-sum payment. |

### Question 3: Focus on Research Ethics & Committees (7 + 4 + 4)

**Main Answer:**
**Ethical Issues in Research:**
Ethical issues arise when researchers prioritize career advancement, fame, or funding over scientific truth and subject safety. Major issues include violating patient confidentiality, exposing animals to unnecessary pain, authorship disputes (Ghost/Gift authorship), and Academic Fraud (FFP).

**1. Data Fabrication (Making it up):**

**2. Data Falsification (Tampering):**
*   *Explanation:* The experiment was conducted, but the researcher actively manipulates the equipment, changes data, or deletes inconvenient outliers to force the results to confirm their specific hypothesis.
*   *Hypothetical Scenario:* A biologist tests a drug intended to shrink cancer cells. In the lab, 8 out of 10 cells shrink, but 2 cells grow massively. Knowing the 2 growing cells will ruin the statistical significance of their paper, the biologist maliciously deletes those 2 records from the final spreadsheet, claiming a "100% success rate."

**(b) Discuss the constitution and role of the Institutional Animal Ethics Committee (IAEC). What guidelines must be followed to prevent animal cruelty in research? (4)**

**Main Answer:**
**Constitution and Role:**
In India, the IAEC operates under the stringent guidelines of CPCSEA (Committee for the Purpose of Control and Supervision of Experiments on Animals). The committee must include biologists, veterinarians, and an independent socially-appointed animal welfare activist. Their role is to review, approve, and halt any research protocols involving animals, ensuring animals are only used when absolutely biologically necessary.

2.  **Reduction:** Statistical proof must be provided that the absolute minimum number of animals is being used to achieve valid results.
3.  **Refinement:** The protocol must ensure that animals receive proper housing, nutrition, and immediate anesthesia/analgesics to minimize distress, and mandate humane euthanasia once the experiment concludes.

**Constitution and Role:**
The **Institutional Review Board (IRB)** (or IEC) is an independent committee comprised of scientists, medical doctors, legal experts, and laypeople from the community. Its exclusive mandate is to protect the rights, safety, dignity, and well-being of human subjects involved in research.

2.  **Vulnerable Populations:** They provide extreme oversight when research involves children, prisoners, or pregnant women to prevent coercion.
3.  **Informed Consent:** They meticulously review the "Informed Consent Form" to ensure it is written in simple, non-legalistic language so that participants truly understand what is being done to their bodies and their absolute right to withdraw.

### Question 4: Focus on Plagiarism & Reproducibility (6 + 5 + 4)

**(a) Define Plagiarism. Discuss the consequences of plagiarism in academic research. Mention the tools/software commonly used to check similarity. (6)**

**Main Answer:**
**Definition:**
**Plagiarism** is the unethical act of taking someone else's original ideas, theories, data, or exact text and passing it off as one's own original work without providing appropriate academic citation.

**Consequences in Academic Research:**
1.  **Retraction:** Journals will formally and publicly "Retract" the plagiarized paper. It remains online with a massive red "RETRACTED" watermark, permanently scarring the researcher's global reputation.
2.  **Academic Penalties:** University professors caught plagiarizing face immediate suspension or termination of their tenured employment. Students face expulsion and degree cancellation.
3.  **Loss of Funding:** Government agencies (like DST or NIH) permanently blacklist plagiarists from ever receiving financial research grants again.

**Tools for Checking Similarity:**
Universities and publishers mandate the use of algorithmic text-matching software. The most prominent tools are **Turnitin**, **iThenticate**, and **Urkund**, which compare the manuscript against a massive global database of published papers and student essays to generate a "Similarity Index percentage".

---

**(b) Why is "Reproducibility" considered the cornerstone of the scientific method? Discuss the factors that lead to non-reproducible research. (5)**

**Main Answer:**
**Cornerstone of Science:**
Science relies on objective truth, not blind faith. If a researcher discovers a breakthrough, the global scientific community cannot accept it as "Fact" unless independent scientists in different labs can repeat the exact experiment and achieve the exact same mathematical result. **Reproducibility** proves that the result is a universal law of nature, not a localized fluke, equipment error, or fraud.

**Factors Leading to Non-Reproducible Research:**
1.  **Poor Methodology Documentation:** The original authors wrote a vague 'Methods' section, failing to specify exact reagent concentrations, incubation times, or software versions.
2.  **p-hacking (Data Dredging):** Running dozens of different statistical tests on random data until a "statistically significant" anomaly appears by pure coincidence, and publishing it as a factual correlation.
3.  **Publication Bias (Hiding Negative Results):** Journals prioritize publishing "successful" experiments. Authors selectively hide failed trials and publish only the one time the experiment worked, skewing reality.

**(c) What is the difference between referencing (citation) and a bibliography? (4)**

**Main Answer:**
| Feature | Referencing (Citations/Reference List) | Bibliography |

### Question 5: Mixed IPR, TRIPS & Publishing Bag (6 + 5 + 4)

**(a) Write a detailed note on the TRIPS agreement under the WTO. How does it harmonize IPR laws across different member countries? (6)**

**Main Answer:**
**The TRIPS Agreement:**
The **Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights (TRIPS)** agreement is a comprehensive international legal treaty established by the World Trade Organization (WTO) in 1995. Prior to TRIPS, intellectual property protection varied wildly; developing nations often refused to recognize Western pharmaceutical patents.

**How it Harmonizes IPR Laws:**
1.  **Universal Minimum Standards:** TRIPS forced all WTO member countries to modify their domestic laws to meet strict, universal baselines. For example, it mandated that *every* country must provide exactly 20 years of protection for patents and minimum copyright protection of "Life + 50 years".
2.  **National Treatment:** It enforces non-discrimination. India cannot grant strong patent protections to Indian citizens while offering weak protections to American inventors operating in India.
3.  **Dispute Settlement:** It established a powerful global court mechanism. If a country tolerates rampant IP piracy, the WTO can authorize severe economic trade sanctions against that country, forcing compliance.

**(b) Explain Copyright law. What rights does it grant to the creator, and what is the concept of "Fair Use" in academic research? (5)**

**Main Answer:**
**Copyright Law:**
Copyright is an automatic legal right granted to the creator of an original literary, artistic, or software work the moment it is fixed in a tangible medium (written down or recorded). It does not require formal registration.

**Rights Granted to the Creator:**
1.  **Economic Rights:** The exclusive right to reproduce, publish, sell, translate, or adapt (e.g., making a movie from a book) the work for financial gain.
2.  **Moral Rights:** The absolute right to be attributed as the author, and the right to object to any derogatory distortion or mutilation of the work that harms their reputation.

**Concept of "Fair Use" (Fair Dealing):**
Because locking down knowledge restricts societal progress, copyright law contains a massive loophole called "Fair Use". It allows scholars, critics, and students to copy and reproduce small, reasonable portions of a copyrighted work *without* asking the author for permission or paying royalties, provided the copying is strictly for **academic research, educational teaching, news reporting, parody, or review**, and does not destroy the commercial market value of the original work.

**(c) Discuss the ethical considerations in determining "Authorship" of a research paper. What is "Ghost Authorship" and "Gift Authorship"? (4)**

**Main Answer:**
**Ethical Considerations for Authorship:**
Authorship is the currency of academia; it determines promotions and funding. According to the ICMJE guidelines, authorship must be restricted strictly to individuals who:
1. Made a substantial intellectual contribution to the concept, design, or data analysis.
2. Actively participated in drafting or critically revising the manuscript.
3. Approved the final version for publication and accepted ethical accountability for it.

**Unethical Authorship Practices:**
1.  **Ghost Authorship:** An unethical practice where a person makes a massive intellectual contribution (or completely writes the paper, often paid by a pharmaceutical company) but their name is deliberately **hidden/omitted** from the author list to make the research look impartial.
2.  **Gift (Guest/Honorary) Authorship:** An unethical practice where an individual's name is added to the author list despite making **absolutely zero intellectual contribution** to the work. This is usually done to flatter a powerful Head of Department or to use a famous scientist's name to increase the chances of journal acceptance.
