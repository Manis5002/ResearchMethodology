# GROUP A: 1-MARK QUESTIONS (SHORT ANSWER)

**Data Collection & Validation:**

**Q. 1. What is Primary Data?**
**Main Answer:** **Primary Data** refers to original, first-hand information collected directly by the researcher specifically for the purpose of their current study (e.g., through surveys or experiments).

**Q. 2. What is Secondary Data?**
**Main Answer:** **Secondary Data** is data that has already been collected and analyzed by someone else for a previous purpose, which a new researcher utilizes for their own study (e.g., census reports or journal articles).

**Q. 3. Mention two key aspects (parameters) of method validation.**
**Main Answer:** Two key parameters of method validation are **Accuracy** (closeness to the true value) and **Precision** (repeatability of the results).

**Q. 4. What is the difference between an Interview and a Questionnaire?**
**Main Answer:** An **Interview** involves direct, real-time verbal questioning between the researcher and respondent, whereas a **Questionnaire** is a printed or digital set of questions filled out independently by the respondent.

**Q. 5. What is Participant Observation?**
**Main Answer:** **Participant Observation** is a qualitative data collection method where the researcher immerses themselves into the daily life or environment of the group being studied to observe their behavior firsthand.

**Sampling Methods:**

**Q. 6. Define 'Population' (Universe) in the context of research.**
**Main Answer:** The **Population** (or Universe) refers to the entire, complete set of individuals, items, or data units that share a common characteristic and are the target of the research study.

**Q. 7. Define a 'Sample'.**
**Main Answer:** A **Sample** is a finite, statistically representative subset or portion drawn from the larger target population for the purpose of empirical investigation.

**Q. 8. What is a Sampling Frame?**
**Main Answer:** A **Sampling Frame** is the actual physical list, directory, or source material containing all the specific units of the population from which the sample is officially drawn (e.g., a voter registry).

**Q. 9. What is the fundamental difference between Probability and Non-Probability sampling?**
**Main Answer:** In **Probability Sampling**, every unit in the population has a known, non-zero, and equal chance of being selected via random selection, whereas **Non-Probability Sampling** relies on the researcher's subjective judgment and convenience.

**Q. 10. Give an example of a Probability sampling method.**
**Main Answer:** **Simple Random Sampling**, where units are chosen using a lottery method or random number generator.

**Q. 11. What is Snowball Sampling?**
**Main Answer:** **Snowball Sampling** is a non-probability technique where existing study subjects recruit or refer future subjects from among their acquaintances, causing the sample size to roll and grow like a snowball.

**Q. 12. What is Purposive (Judgmental) Sampling?**
**Main Answer:** **Purposive Sampling** is a non-probability method where the researcher uses their own expert judgment to deliberately select specific subjects who represent the most typical or relevant units for the study.

**Data Processing, Packages & Hypothesis:**

**Q. 13. What is Data Coding?**
**Main Answer:** **Data Coding** is the process of assigning numerical values or symbols to qualitative survey responses (e.g., Male = 1, Female = 2) to facilitate easy entry into statistical software.

**Q. 14. What is Data Tabulation?**
**Main Answer:** **Data Tabulation** is the orderly and systematic presentation of processed numerical data into logical columns and rows to facilitate visual analysis and comparison.

**Q. 15. Name two popular statistical software packages used for data analysis.**
**Main Answer:** Two highly popular packages are **SPSS** (Statistical Package for the Social Sciences) and **Sigma STAT**.

**Q. 16. What is a Null Hypothesis ($H_0$)?**
**Main Answer:** The **Null Hypothesis ($H_0$)** is the default assumption stating that there is **no significant difference, no effect, or no relationship** between the variables being studied.

**Q. 17. What is an Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$ or $H_a$)?**
**Main Answer:** The **Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$)** is the statement that actively contradicts the null hypothesis, asserting that there **is** a statistically significant effect, difference, or relationship between the variables.

**Q. 18. Define a Type I Error in hypothesis testing.**
**Main Answer:** A **Type I Error** (False Positive) occurs when the researcher mistakenly **rejects a true Null Hypothesis** (i.e., claiming an effect exists when it actually does not).

**Q. 19. Define a Type II Error in hypothesis testing.**
**Main Answer:** A **Type II Error** (False Negative) occurs when the researcher mistakenly **accepts (fails to reject) a false Null Hypothesis** (i.e., missing an effect that actually exists).

**Q. 20. When is a Student's t-test generally used?**
**Main Answer:** A **Student's t-test** is typically used to compare the means of exactly **two groups** to determine if they are statistically significantly different from each other, especially when the sample size is small ($n < 30$).

**Q. 21. What does the acronym ANOVA stand for?**
**Main Answer:** ANOVA stands for **Analysis of Variance**.

**Q. 22. What is the "Level of Significance" (Alpha $\alpha$) typically set at in standard research?**
**Main Answer:** The **Level of Significance ($\alpha$)** is conventionally set at **0.05 (5%)** in standard social and scientific research, or **0.01 (1%)** for highly critical medical research.

**Q. 2. What are the essential aspects of method validation? Briefly explain Accuracy, Precision, and Robustness.**
**Introduction:**
**Method Validation** is the process of mathematically proving that a specific analytical or data-collection procedure yields reliable, reproducible, and scientifically valid results before deploying it in the actual study.

**Main Answer:**
The essential parameters of method validation include:
1.  **Accuracy (Trueness):** 
    *   It measures how close the experimental or survey results are to the absolute "true" or accepted reference value. 
    *   *Example:* If a scale reads 5.0 kg for a perfectly calibrated 5.0 kg weight, it is highly accurate.
2.  **Precision (Repeatability):** 
    *   It measures the degree of agreement or consistency among a series of measurements taken from the exact same sample under identical conditions. A method can be precise without being accurate.
3.  **Robustness:**
    *   It evaluates the reliability of an analytical method when subjected to small, deliberate variations in method parameters (e.g., minor changes in temperature, pH, or survey administration time). A robust method remains unaffected by these tiny operational fluctuations.

**Conclusion:**
Without rigorous validation of these three aspects, the collected data will be plagued by systemic errors, rendering the entire research conclusion scientifically invalid.

**Q. 3. Discuss "Observation" as a method of data collection. What are its merits and demerits?**

Observation is unparalleled for deep, behavioral psychology studies, but is generally supplemented with interviews to understand the "why" behind the observed actions.

**Q. 4. Write a short note on the design of a good Questionnaire. What types of questions should be avoided?**

A **Questionnaire** is a structured instrument consisting of a series of questions to gather data. A poorly designed questionnaire leads to survey abandonment and corrupted data.

A scientifically valid questionnaire relies heavily on closed-ended, neutral, and unambiguous questions to guarantee reliable, easily tabulated statistical data.

**Q. 5. Explain the concept of Probability Sampling. Briefly discuss Simple Random Sampling and Stratified Random Sampling.**
**Introduction:**
**Probability Sampling** is a scientific technique where every single unit in the target population has a known, non-zero, and equal mathematical chance of being selected, eliminating human bias.

**Main Answer:**
1.  **Simple Random Sampling (SRS):**
    *   *Concept:* The purest form of sampling. Every member of the population is assigned a number. A sample is drawn completely blindly using a random number generator or a lottery system.
    *   *Application:* Best used when the population is small, highly homogeneous, and an exact sampling frame (list) is available.
2.  **Stratified Random Sampling:**
    *   *Concept:* Used when the population is heterogeneous (diverse). The population is first mathematically divided into mutually exclusive sub-groups called **Strata** (e.g., dividing a university by major: Engineering, Arts, Business). 
    *   *Process:* A Simple Random Sample is then drawn independently from *each* stratum in proportion to its size.
    *   *Application:* Guarantees that minority groups are perfectly represented in the final sample, reducing sampling error significantly compared to SRS.

**Conclusion:**
Probability sampling ensures maximum statistical representativeness, allowing the researcher to accurately generalize the sample findings back to the entire population.

**Q. 6. What is Non-Probability Sampling? Explain Convenience Sampling and Snowball Sampling.**
**Introduction:**
**Non-Probability Sampling** is a technique where the selection of units is based entirely on the researcher's subjective judgment, convenience, or accessibility, meaning not all population units have a chance of being selected.

**Main Answer:**
1.  **Convenience Sampling:**
    *   *Concept:* The researcher selects the sample simply because they are physically and temporally easiest to access.
    *   *Example:* A professor using the 30 students sitting in their own morning class to fill out a psychological survey.
    *   *Drawback:* Highly biased and mathematically unrepresentative of the global population.
2.  **Snowball Sampling:**
    *   *Concept:* A chain-referral system. The researcher finds a few initial subjects who fit the criteria. After interviewing them, the researcher asks them to recommend/recruit other people they know who fit the same profile.
    *   *Example:* Researching illegal drug users or underground hackers. Since no public "list" (sampling frame) exists, finding one user leads to finding the entire hidden network.

**Conclusion:**
While statistically flawed for making generalized population estimates, non-probability sampling is the absolute best approach for exploratory research and studying rare or hidden communities.

**Q. 7. Differentiate between Stratified Sampling and Cluster Sampling.**
**Introduction:**
Both Stratified and Cluster sampling are probability techniques that involve dividing the population into sub-groups. However, their internal logic and application are completely opposite.

**Main Answer:**
| Feature | Stratified Sampling | Cluster Sampling |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Nature of Groups** | Groups (Strata) are artificially created by the researcher based on specific traits (e.g., Age, Income). | Groups (Clusters) are naturally occurring geographic or organizational blocks (e.g., City blocks, Schools). |
| **Internal Homogeneity** | Elements **within** a stratum are highly **Homogeneous** (similar). | Elements **within** a cluster are highly **Heterogeneous** (diverse). |
| **External Heterogeneity**| Different strata are **Heterogeneous** to each other. | Different clusters are **Homogeneous** to each other (they look like mini-populations). |
| **Selection Process** | A random sample of units is selected from **EVERY** stratum. | The researcher randomly selects a few **ENTIRE** clusters and surveys every single unit inside them. |
| **Primary Goal** | To increase statistical precision and ensure minority representation. | To drastically reduce travel costs and administrative time over massive geographical areas. |

**Conclusion:**
Choose stratified sampling for precision across demographics, and cluster sampling for logistical feasibility across massive, dispersed populations.

**Q. 10. Outline the standard procedure for Hypothesis Testing (from stating the hypothesis to making a decision).**
**Introduction:**
Hypothesis testing is a rigid, formal statistical procedure used by researchers to determine whether there is enough evidence in a sample of data to infer a specific conclusion for the entire population.

**Main Answer:**
**Procedure Steps:**
1.  **State the Hypotheses:** Explicitly formulate the Null Hypothesis ($H_0$ - stating no effect) and the Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$ - stating an effect exists).
2.  **Select the Level of Significance ($\alpha$):** Set the risk threshold for committing a Type I error. Universally, this is set at $\alpha = 0.05$ (5%).
3.  **Select the Statistical Test:** Choose the appropriate mathematical tool based on the data type (e.g., T-test for 2 groups, ANOVA for 3+ groups, Chi-Square for categorical data).
4.  **Calculate the Test Statistic & p-value:** Run the data through software to generate the mathematical test statistic and the exact $p$-value.
5.  **Make the Decision:** Compare the $p$-value against $\alpha$.
    *   If **$p \le 0.05$**, **Reject** the Null Hypothesis.
    *   If **$p > 0.05$**, **Accept** (fail to reject) the Null Hypothesis.

**Conclusion:**
This strict, emotionless procedure prevents scientists from drawing conclusions based on intuition or coincidence.

**Q. 11. Explain the difference between a Type I Error and a Type II Error with a real-world example.**
**Introduction:**
Because hypothesis testing relies on a limited *sample* to make assumptions about an entire *population*, it is never 100% accurate. The system is inherently vulnerable to two specific types of mathematical errors.

**Main Answer:**
| Feature | Type I Error ($\alpha$ / False Positive) | Type II Error ($\beta$ / False Negative) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Definition** | The researcher mistakenly **rejects** a Null Hypothesis ($H_0$) that is actually **True**. | The researcher mistakenly **accepts** a Null Hypothesis ($H_0$) that is actually **False**. |
| **The Action** | Claiming a result or effect exists when it is just a random coincidence. | Missing a real, genuine effect and claiming nothing happened. |
| **Severity** | Usually considered the more severe error in science (claiming a useless drug cures cancer). | Considered less severe, though still damaging (missing a drug that actually works). |

**Real-World Example (Medical Test):**
Let $H_0$ be "The patient is Healthy."
*   **Type I Error (False Positive):** The doctor tells the healthy patient they have cancer. (Rejecting a true $H_0$). Causes massive panic and unnecessary chemotherapy.
*   **Type II Error (False Negative):** The doctor tells a patient who actually has cancer that they are perfectly healthy. (Accepting a false $H_0$). The cancer goes untreated.

**Q. 12. Compare the Student's t-test and ANOVA. Under what conditions is ANOVA preferred over a t-test?**
**Introduction:**
Both the t-test and ANOVA are parametric statistical tests used to compare the means of different groups to check for statistically significant differences. 

**Main Answer:**
| Feature | Student's t-test | ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Number of Groups** | Strictly limited to comparing the means of **exactly TWO groups**. | Capable of comparing the means of **THREE or more groups** simultaneously. |
| **Test Statistic Used** | Yields a **$t$-statistic**. | Yields an **$F$-ratio**. |
| **Example** | Comparing the test scores of Male students vs. Female students. | Comparing the test scores of students from Science, Arts, and Commerce streams. |

**Why ANOVA is Preferred (The Fatal Flaw of Multiple t-tests):**
If a researcher has 4 groups (A, B, C, D) and tries to use t-tests, they would have to run 6 separate tests (A-B, A-C, A-D, B-C, B-D, C-D). Every single test carries a 5% risk of a Type I error ($\alpha = 0.05$). Running multiple t-tests mathematically compounds this risk, inflating the **Family-Wise Error Rate** to nearly 30% (meaning a false positive is almost guaranteed). 
**ANOVA** tests all groups simultaneously in one single calculation, perfectly maintaining the error risk at a strict 5%.

### Question 2: Focus on Sampling Methods (7 + 8)

**(a) Define Sample Design. What are the characteristics of a "Good Sample Design"? Explain the concept of Sampling Error. (7)**

**Main Answer:**
**Sample Design Definition:**
A **Sample Design** is the definite, pre-calculated architectural blueprint determined by the researcher *before* any data is collected, dictating exactly how a representative subset (sample) will be drawn from a vast population.

**Characteristics of a "Good Sample Design":**
1.  **Truly Representative:** The selected sample must perfectly mirror the demographic, financial, and structural characteristics of the entire population.
2.  **Small Sampling Error:** The mathematical design must guarantee that the margin of error remains as tight as possible.
3.  **Economically Viable:** The design must optimize the sample size to yield accurate results without exceeding the available budget and time constraints.
4.  **Control of Systematic Bias:** It must mathematically eliminate the researcher's personal prejudices from the selection process.

**Concept of Sampling Error:**
Because a sample is only a fraction of the population, its results will never be 100% perfectly identical to studying the entire universe. The **Sampling Error** is the mathematical difference between the *Sample Statistic* (e.g., the average income of 500 surveyed people) and the *True Population Parameter* (the actual average income of all 10 million citizens). 
*Crucial Rule:* Sampling error can never be fully eliminated, but it is mathematically guaranteed to decrease as the sample size ($n$) increases.

**(b) Categorize and explain the different types of Probability and Non-Probability sampling methods. In exploratory research, which category is generally preferred and why? (8)**

**Main Answer:**
**Classification of Sampling Methods:**

**(a) Detail the steps involved in Hypothesis Testing. Clearly explain the concepts of Null Hypothesis, Alternative Hypothesis, Level of Significance, and Critical Region. (7)**

**Main Answer:**
**Steps in Hypothesis Testing:**
1.  **State the Hypotheses:** Formulate the $H_0$ and $H_1$.
2.  **Set the Level of Significance ($\alpha$):** Usually 0.05.
3.  **Choose the Test Statistic:** (e.g., Z-test, t-test, ANOVA) based on the sample size and data type.
4.  **Determine the Critical Region:** Find the boundary value on the statistical table.
5.  **Compute the Statistic:** Run the data to calculate the exact mathematical test value.
6.  **Make the Decision:** If the computed value falls inside the critical region, Reject $H_0$. Otherwise, Accept $H_0$.

**Core Concepts:**
*   **Null Hypothesis ($H_0$):** The default, skeptical assumption that nothing new is happening. It states there is **zero difference** or **zero correlation** between variables (e.g., "The new drug has no effect on blood pressure").
*   **Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$):** The active claim the researcher is trying to mathematically prove. It states an effect **does** exist (e.g., "The new drug lowers blood pressure significantly").
*   **Level of Significance ($\alpha$):** The maximum accepted probability of committing a Type I error (rejecting a true null hypothesis). If $\alpha = 0.05$, the researcher accepts a 5% risk of being completely wrong.
*   **Critical Region (Rejection Region):** The extreme tail ends of the probability curve. If the calculated test statistic lands deep inside this extreme area, the result is deemed mathematically impossible to be a mere coincidence, forcing the rejection of $H_0$.

**(b) What is the p-value in statistical testing? How is the p-value used to accept or reject the Null Hypothesis? (4)**

**Main Answer:**
**Definition of p-value:**
The **p-value** (probability value) is the exact mathematical probability of obtaining test results at least as extreme as the ones observed during the experiment, assuming that the Null Hypothesis ($H_0$) is absolutely true.

**The Decision Mechanism:**
The p-value acts as the ultimate truth-metric in modern software-driven research, compared directly against the chosen Level of Significance ($\alpha$, usually 0.05).
*   **If p-value $\le \alpha$ (e.g., p = 0.02):** The probability that the results occurred by pure random coincidence is only 2%. Because this is less than the 5% risk threshold, the researcher **Rejects the Null Hypothesis**. The results are deemed statistically significant.
*   **If p-value $> \alpha$ (e.g., p = 0.40):** There is a massive 40% chance the results are just a random coincidence. The researcher must **Accept (fail to reject) the Null Hypothesis**. There is no statistical significance.

### Question 5: Focus on Statistical Tests (t-test & ANOVA) (6 + 5 + 4)

**(a) What is a Student’s t-test? Differentiate between an Independent Samples t-test and a Paired (Dependent) Samples t-test with examples. (6)**

**Main Answer:**
**Student's t-test:** It is a parametric statistical tool used to determine if there is a mathematically significant difference between the means (averages) of **exactly two groups**, specifically designed for small sample sizes ($n < 30$) where the population standard deviation is unknown.

**Differentiation:**
1.  **Independent Samples t-test:**
    *   *Concept:* Compares the means of two completely different, unrelated, independent groups of subjects.
    *   *Example:* Comparing the final exam scores of **Class A (taught by Teacher X)** against **Class B (taught by Teacher Y)** to see which teaching method is statistically superior.
2.  **Paired (Dependent) Samples t-test:**
    *   *Concept:* Compares the means of the **exact same group of subjects**, measured twice at two different points in time (usually a "Before" and "After" scenario).
    *   *Example:* Measuring the blood pressure of 20 patients **before** giving them a new drug, and measuring those exact same 20 patients **after** taking the drug to see if a significant drop occurred.

**(b) Explain the concept of Analysis of Variance (ANOVA). Why can't we just use multiple t-tests instead of a One-Way ANOVA when comparing three or more groups? (5)**

**Main Answer:**
**Concept of ANOVA:**
**Analysis of Variance (ANOVA)** is a robust parametric test used to compare the means of **three or more independent groups** simultaneously to determine if at least one group mean is statistically significantly different from the others. It calculates an **F-ratio** by dividing the variance *between* the groups by the variance *within* the groups.

**Why Not Use Multiple t-tests? (The Fatal Flaw):**
If a researcher has 4 groups (A, B, C, D) and wishes to compare them using t-tests, they must run 6 individual tests. 
Every individual t-test carries a strict 5% risk of committing a Type I Error (False Positive). 
*   **The Family-Wise Error Rate:** When running multiple sequential t-tests, the mathematical probability of a false positive drastically compounds. Running 6 t-tests artificially inflates the overall error risk from 5% to nearly **26%**. This guarantees that the researcher will falsely claim a significant difference that does not exist.
*   **The ANOVA Solution:** ANOVA analyzes all groups simultaneously in a single, unified equation, perfectly maintaining the overall Type I Error risk at a strict, safe 5%.

**(c) Differentiate between One-Way ANOVA and Two-Way ANOVA. (4)**

**Main Answer:**
| Feature | One-Way ANOVA | Two-Way ANOVA |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Number of Independent Variables** | Analyzes the impact of exactly **ONE** independent variable (factor) on the dependent variable. | Analyzes the impact of exactly **TWO** independent variables (factors) simultaneously. |
| **Objective** | To test for the Main Effect of the single factor across 3+ groups. | To test for the Main Effects of both factors, AND the critical **Interaction Effect** between them. |
| **Example Scenario** | Testing how different **Diets** (Vegan, Keto, Paleo) affect a person's weight loss. | Testing how different **Diets** (Vegan, Keto) AND different **Exercise Routines** (Cardio, Weights) interact to affect a person's weight loss. |
